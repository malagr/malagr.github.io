REISE, H., HUTCHINSON, J.M.C., SCHUNACK, S. & SCHLITT, B. 2011. Folia Malacologica 19: 201-223.

Deroceras panormitanum and congeners from Malta and Sicily, with a redescription of the widespread pest slug as Deroceras invadens n. sp.

In this paper we describe the anatomy and mating behaviour of three Deroceras species. One is D. invadens, formally described and named in the paper, but familiar right round the world as the tramp species previously known as D. panormitanum or D. caruanae. Another is the true D. panormitanum, known only from Sicily and Malta. The third is D. golcheri, the only other species known from Malta, which the mtDNA implies is a close relative of D. panormitanum. The abstract and links to download the full pdf are here.

Here we additionally provide the video recordings of copulations, the evidence that most clearly demonstrates the significance of the differences between these species.

Mating of Deroceras invadens

Early courtship of D. invadens. N.B. this video is not speeded up! Once both partners protrude their sarcobela, sometimes followed by a short period of peace, there is a spectacular period of lungeing towards the partner with jaws extended ready to bite. This recording starts 2 min after the second slug has protruded its sarcobelum. It is probably not incidental that the sarcobelum also contacts the partner during these lunges. Often the target is the partner’s tail, which leads to dramatic tail lashing. The flag-like configuration of the tip of the tail, is already present during precourtship. Following the partner’s tail leads to the formation of a circle that rotates, but this may be briefly disrupted by lunges towards the middle and head end of the partner. The avi file is twice the dimensions. Slugs are from Cala di Luna, Sardinia, but this aspect of their mating is representative also of specimens from the type locality. Copulation of D. invadens. Prior to copulation each mouth acts on the sarcobelum base of the partner, but mouth-to-mouth contact is lacking (in contrast with the true D. panormitanum shown below; nor are the heads raised so far off the ground). Each penial caecum everts quite slowly and deposits the sperm on the partner’s sarcobelum. After the caecae have retracted there is a pause, often longer than in this mating, when the penes remain pressed against each other pulsating. This lull is broken when the penial lobe of one partner everts, and then the long fingers of the penial gland evert to cover the other partner. In the mating shown here, the first penial lobe to evert is initially hard to spot, but the eversion is clearer with the second partner, which everts its penial lobe as the first partner is already everting its penial gland: lack of synchrony is typical at this stage. From the start of copulation until the second partner everts its tentacles again lasts over 5 minutes. Slugs were from the type locality. Click on picture below to start/stop video (1.3 Mb)

Copulation of D. invadens from Sardinia. The main differences to typical copulations of slugs from the type locality are that the penial lobe starts to evert much earlier, even while the caecum is still extended, and the eversion of the penial glands does not follow directly after that of the caecum. The penial lobe also everts in a more posterior direction, in this respect more like the true D. panormitanum, as shown below. The same mating as Fig. 6r-t in the paper. Copulation of D. panormitanum. These two three-minute videos shows two views of a single copulation. Note the mouth-to-mouth and then mouth-to-sarcobelum-base contact immediately before copulation as the animals twist round each other and rear up. The eversion of the genitalia is very fast (the avi files have a higher frame rate): the tentacle-like penial caecum reaches round the back of the partner’s sarcobelum whilst the prickley balloon-like penial lobe points backwards towards the animal’s own sarcobelum. Once the caecum retracts, the penial glands evert. Then the mantles start oscillating side-to-side, and the copious mucus production makes the animals appear to melt together as they push against each other and again rear up. The sarcobela retract at this stage and the animals eventually separate. Same mating as shown for the mid-courtship sequence.

Mating of Deroceras golcheri

Copulation. This 2 min video shows the copulation. When the swollen bases of the two sarcobela touch the heads entwine (causing the sarcobela to rotate like the hands of a clock) at the same time as the rest of the penis everts. The eversion is slower than in the other species and produces a broad flap. The flap of each partner overlaps that of the other and this is where sperm is transferred. Note the lobe-like extension of the flap that weakly hooks with that of the partner. The finger-like extension of the penis everting to the left produces the penial glands that evert over the partner. Once the penes disengage, the deposited sperm masses are clearly visible. At this stage the mantles oscillate side to side. The same mating as in Fig. 8 in the paper; animals were collected from the type locality (Il Maqluba, Malta). Some notes on the video processing

It has taken me a while to find out about video processing. There is lots of advice on the WEB and some excellent free programs. I describe what currently works best for me. But it is a fiddly process, partly because the best trade-off between quality and file size is a value judgement, and it takes me much too much time. Anyhow, maybe these notes will help others with a similar job to do.

To capture the videos I mostly use an ISIS TFS-0406 digital video recording card. This can save clips as avi files with a resolution of 704×480 and at up to 25 fps. For older video on tape, I digitise using a Plextor ConvertX PX-M402U, which produces 720×576 25 fps avi files.

I use VirtualDub to:

  • 1) select the best sequence (starting at existing keyframes);
  • 2) to adjust contrast, brightness (levels filter: lovely tool), saturation and colour balance (HSV filter: less effective);
  • 3) to resize the images so that they have the correct aspect ratio (pixels from standard video are not square, but displays on computer screens have square pixels: so 704×480 → 640×480) and manageable file size;
  • 4) to include some temporal smoothing (reduces background flicker and increases compressibility; it is possible to apply this just to a part of the video — blending — so as to leave high-speed portions unsmoothed);
  • 5) adjust frame rate (e.g. dropping every other frame to lower file size and/or playing frames faster to speed up the playback);
  • 6) select what parts of the picture I will crop off (but cropping is done later in Avisynth, after filtering);
  • 7) add any labels (using the subtitler plugin written by the same author as VirtualDub, and editing the ssa files using WordPad).

I then save the output using lossless compression (HuffYUV in the ffdshow codec) and further process these files using an Avisynth script (run in VirtualDub again; it is in this second run through VirtualDub, after filtering, that I add labels). I use Avisynth to:

  • 1) apply spatial filters, in particular VagueDenoiser and RemoveGrain (useful to increase compressibility, to filter out noise in the background, and to lower the contrast of some highlights);
  • 2) crop;
  • 3) where necessary, overlay a mask to hide the distracting clock counter in the corner of the original;
  • 4) where appropriate put two videos alongside one another (StackHorizontal() or StackVertical()), adding a border to separate, turn, etc.;
  • 5) compare the effect of different filters and compressions using StackHorizontal().

To produce the xvid avi files, I use the ffdshow codec in VirtualDub. I find that in an initial one-pass compression, 92% quality is about right not to lose much quality. I then use two-pass compression to adjust the file sizes slightly downwards depending on how the quality looks.

I also produce flv files to run on this web page itself. My contraint here is that I want the video not to be too large (if possible less than 1 MB, so that it is just about usable on a dial-up link), but the important features to be recognisable; if users find the quality unattractive, they have option of downloading the higher-quality xvid files. I use the original flv encoding (i.e. H.263, rather than H.264 or VP6) as this is compatible with earlier versions of the Flash Player. To produce the flv files, I resave the output from VirtualDub as HuffYUV again. To convert to flv I have formerly used the program Super©, but the current version (48) throws up lots of error messages as it runs on my computer (they seem not to matter, but annoying). So I now tend instead to run ffmpeg in a DOS window. A typical script is
    ffmpeg.exe -i infile.avi -an -g 64 -r 6.25 -y -vcodec flv -qscale 7 -pass 1 outfile.flv
and I also use
    ffmpeg.exe -i outfile.flv -f image2 -vframes 1 outfile.jpg
to generate a jpg file of the first frame, which I then resave with Coreldraw at a higher compression. For displaying the flv files on this page I use JW FLV Media Player 4.7 , embedding using the static method of swfobject 2.2. This is far from the latest version of the JW Player but I know that it works with not-the-most-recent versions of the Flash Player, and I didn’t want to have to learn new syntax.
